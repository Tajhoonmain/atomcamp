# Tactical patterns and the psychology behind them

## Mirroring and labeling (tactical empathy)
Repeating the last few words the other side said ("a six-week migration?") draws
out more information for free. Labeling their emotion ("it sounds like timing is
the real constraint") makes them feel understood and lowers their guard. Both
buy information and trust without conceding anything.

## Calibrated questions
"How am I supposed to do that?" and "What about this works for you?" shift the
burden of solving the problem onto the other side without confrontation. They
feel in control while you steer.

## Splitting the difference is usually a trap
A rushed "let's just meet in the middle" averages two anchors and rewards the
side that anchored more aggressively. Slow down, attach conditions, and trade.

## The power of "no" and "that's right"
Getting the other side to say "that's right" (not "you're right") signals real
agreement. Letting them say "no" early gives them a sense of safety and control,
which paradoxically opens them up to your proposal.

## Deadlines, scarcity, and good-cop/bad-cop
Recognize manufactured urgency, an absent "approver" who must sign off, and the
sudden friendly partner after a harsh one. Naming the tactic out loud — calmly —
neutralizes most of its force.

## Multiple equivalent simultaneous offers (MESO)
Presenting two or three packages of equal value to you reveals the other side's
priorities by which one they gravitate to, and reframes the conversation from
"yes/no" to "which".
